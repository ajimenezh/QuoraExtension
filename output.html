If you're actually interested in the company and the position, you should ask questions that will truly help you understand what your life would be like if you worked there, and more importantly how it might be different than your life today.

Perhaps it's a recruiting pet peeve I've picked up over the years, but I have a really hard time buying into a candidate that doesn't seem to be thinking about things that way. If you take a new job, your life will almost certainly be different than it is today.

  • Ask about people : depending on how many people you've already met, surely you've made observations/assumptions about them. Ask your interviewer about those people. What has his/her experience been working with them? Who does he/she work with most closely? Who are the 'go to' people on the team? Why? Answers to these questions will tell you a lot about some of the more subtle dynamics in the office, and allow you to really think about being part of the team.
  • Ask about product : depending on the product (or service) this company provides, these people all probably have a consistent 'company line' perspective, but if you delve a little deeper with each individual you'll probably find answers that resonate a lot closer to your own. Ask how they perceived the product prior to joining and how they perceive it now? Ask what the challenges are to influence product direction (esp if you're an engineer or any role close to the product)? Ask if you're expected to influence product direction if that's not your primary role? Generally any info you can get beyond what you've read in the press or on the company's web page is going to be valuable info to help inform your decision.
  • Ask about impact : this has become a bit of a buzz word that a lot of companies are using to attract new employees, so again it's about separating the party line and discussing it with an individual. If you ask someone for an example of how someone has really made an impact, it's interesting to see if they choose an example of someone else versus something they have actually done. Of course, if you ask 3 people this and they all give you the same example, chances are it's more of a party line example.

There are many more questions that could be added to this list (especially about longer term career growth, etc), but it really depends on what you're looking for and what type of job it is. My best advice is to ask questions that show them how much you're trying to understand what your life would be like in this role, in this company, and how you can actually be successful.

If you do it right, the company will think more highly of you, but more importantly you'll have the right information to make an informed decision about whether it's right for you.
